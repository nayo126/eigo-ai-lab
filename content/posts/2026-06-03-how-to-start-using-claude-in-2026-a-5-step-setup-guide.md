If you've spent time in ChatGPT but never opened Claude, you're not alone. Search around for AI tools that actually earn their keep and Anthropic's Claude comes up constantly — and for good reason. Right now it's one of the strongest models out there for natural writing and clean code.

The friction is in the details. Where's the real site? How far does the free tier get you? And what's the actual difference versus ChatGPT? Here's the practical version: how to sign up, what each plan unlocks, and how to fold Claude into paid work — without the hand-holding.

## The short version: Claude is free to start

Go to **claude.ai**, sign up with an email address or Google account, and you're using it the same day. No credit card required.

The free plan gives you a daily allotment of the latest model, Claude Sonnet 4.6 — enough to test it on real tasks before you spend anything.

You've got three ways in:

- **Web (claude.ai)** — runs entirely in the browser. Lowest friction.
- **Desktop app** — Mac and Windows. Keep it resident and call it up with a shortcut.
- **Mobile app** — iOS and Android. Good for drafting on the move.

Start with the web version. If you add the desktop or mobile app later, your conversation history syncs across all of them under the same account.

## Setting up in 5 steps

If you've registered for anything online, this takes about five minutes.

1. **Go to claude.ai** — type the URL directly rather than clicking a search result. Avoids phishing clones.
2. **Hit "Sign up"** — email or Google account, your call.
3. **Enter the verification code** — the six-digit code lands in your inbox.
4. **Set your name and intended use** — work or personal.
5. **Start typing in the chat box** — that's it.

The first screen is deliberately bare: one input field in the middle. Drop a prompt in and you get a response immediately.

One thing worth knowing early — **model switching**. There's a model selector near the input box. For quick lookups, the fast **Haiku** model is fine. For longer writing or anything involving code, switch to **Sonnet 4.6**, or **Opus** for the heaviest reasoning. Matching the model to the task gets you the right balance of speed and quality instead of waiting on overkill for trivial questions.

## Free vs Pro vs Max

Anthropic splits this into three tiers:

- **Free** — Sonnet 4.6, capped at a daily message limit. Fine for trying it out and light tasks.
- **Pro (~$20/month)** — a much higher message cap, access to Opus and other top models, plus **Projects**.
- **Max** — several times Pro's usage ceiling. Built for people running the model hard all day.

The decision is straightforward. Kicking the tires on weekends? Free is plenty. Using it daily for writing, research, or coding? You'll hit the free cap fast and stall mid-task — Pro becomes the realistic floor.

The feature that earns Pro its keep for freelancers is **Projects**. You can save recurring instructions and reference docs at the project level, so you stop re-pasting setup every session. Define your blog's voice and style rules once, and every draft after that comes out in the same tone. Same for a client's brand guidelines, a codebase's conventions, or a standard brief format.

If you live in a terminal, there's also **Claude Code** — a tool that writes and edits code directly from the command line. It's tied to the Pro and Max plans, and it's a genuinely different way of working: you point it at a repo and describe what you want rather than copy-pasting snippets back and forth.

## Putting Claude to work on paid projects

Claude's real edge is handling long context without falling apart. It holds a 200K-token window — roughly 150,000 words — so you can drop entire documents in and it keeps the thread. That changes what's worth automating.

Three workflows that pay off:

- **Drafting articles and posts** — outline through full draft in one pass. Whether you publish on Substack, Medium, or your own blog, the long-form coherence holds up.
- **Summarizing research** — paste in a long PDF or a wall of source articles and pull out the points that matter.
- **Proposals and outreach** — spin up tailored Upwork pitches or client emails in a fraction of the time.

The single biggest lever on output quality: **set the role and constraints up front**. Something like:

```
You are a professional web copywriter.
The reader is a 20-something just starting a side hustle.
For the keyword "how to start working from home,"
draft a 2,000-word article outline with section headings.
```

Specify who the model is, who it's writing for, and how long the output should be, and the quality jumps. This isn't a Claude quirk — across user communities the consensus is the same: tightly scoped prompts produce more stable results than vague ones.

The mistake that bites people: shipping generated text as-is. There's always a chance of factual errors, so verify every number, name, and date yourself. The people actually making money with this treat AI as the draft writer and themselves as the final editor. That division of labor is the whole game.

## What to watch out for before you lean on it

The upside is real, but so are the failure modes.

**Accuracy first.** Claude answers fluently, and fluent isn't the same as correct. Recent regulations, statistics, and anything time-sensitive are where it's most likely to be wrong. Build the habit of confirming against the primary source or official announcement.

**Confidential data.** Don't paste customer records, passwords, or anything you wouldn't want leaving your machine. If you're using it for client or company work, check the relevant policies and your plan's data settings first — Anthropic's API and business tiers have different data-handling defaults than the consumer apps.

**Copyright and terms.** If you're using output commercially, read the usage terms. They get updated, so check the link in the claude.ai footer periodically rather than assuming what was true last year still holds.

Stay inside those lines and Claude is a safe, genuinely powerful tool to have in the stack.

## Bottom line

Getting started with Claude is nothing more than going to claude.ai and signing up for free. Test Sonnet 4.6 on the free tier first; once it's part of your daily routine, move to Pro — no wasted spend in between.

To make it pay, write prompts that spell out role and constraints, let it handle the draft, and do the fact-checking yourself. Create the account today and throw your first real question at it. The fastest way to know if it fits your workflow is to use it on something you're actually working on right now.

## FAQ

**How far does the free plan get you?**
Free gives you Sonnet 4.6 for a few dozen messages a day — enough for writing, summarizing, and code. No card needed. Hit the cap and it refills within a few hours. Pro (~$20/month) raises the ceiling roughly 5x.

**Claude or ChatGPT for writing?**
Claude has the edge on natural long-form prose and holding context, which makes it strong for articles and email. The 200K-token window (~150K words) means it handles big documents in one shot. ChatGPT wins on image generation and its broader plugin/tool ecosystem. For most writing-heavy work, Claude.

**Is there a mobile app?**
Yes — free official apps on iOS and Android. Log in with the same account and your history syncs with the web version. Voice input and image reading both work on mobile.

**Is the writing quality production-ready?**
For English it's well past usable — Sonnet 4.6 produces clean, natural prose, including formal business tone, and will draft a multi-thousand-word piece in under a minute. It's fully viable for client writing work, as long as you handle the final fact-check.
