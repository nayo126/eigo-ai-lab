You want to try AI image generation, but every tool wants an email, a credit card, or a desktop install before it'll render a single pixel. Annoying. There's a tool that skips all of that: open a browser tab, type a prompt, hit a button. That's **DALL·E mini** — except it doesn't go by that name anymore. Today it's called **Craiyon**.

Below is exactly how it works, why the name changed, how to write prompts that don't come out as blurry mush, what the free tier actually costs you, and where it fits if you're trying to make money with generated images.

## TL;DR: DALL·E mini is now Craiyon, and it runs in your browser with zero setup

The whole workflow is "open the site, type in English, click generate." That's it. The tool is fully browser-based by design — nothing to download, nothing to configure.

One thing to clear up first: **DALL·E mini was renamed to Craiyon back in 2022.** OpenAI asked the project to stop using the DALL·E name to avoid confusion with their official models, so developer Boris Dayma — who built and still runs it independently — rebranded it. So if you search "dall e mini" today, you land on `craiyon.com`.

This is *not* OpenAI's DALL·E 3. The image quality and prompt accuracy are noticeably lower than the official models. What you get instead:

- **Free with effectively unlimited generations** (the free plan has ads and a queue)
- No email, no credit card, no account
- Multiple candidate images per generation, so you pick from a batch

It's the lowest-friction way to find out what AI image generation feels like before you commit money or time to anything heavier.

## How to use Craiyon: 5 steps

The flow is genuinely simple:

1. Open `craiyon.com` in any browser.
2. Type what you want to see in the center text box — **in English**.
3. Click **DRAW** (the generate button).
4. Wait. After roughly a minute on the free plan, a grid of candidate images appears.
5. Click the one you like to open and save it.

The only real gotcha is the input box. Craiyon technically accepts other languages, but it was trained predominantly on English data, so **you'll get results far closer to what you pictured if you write the prompt in English.**

Say you want "a dog running on the beach at sunset." Don't overthink it — write:

```
a dog running on the beach at sunset, photorealistic
```

That trailing style tag does a lot of work. Swap `photorealistic` for `anime style`, `oil painting`, or `watercolor` and the entire output shifts. If your English is shaky, run your idea through DeepL or any translator first and paste the result — the model never knows the difference. Free-tier generations can take a minute or more under load, so don't panic if it's slow.

## Writing prompts that actually produce what you want

The most common reaction to Craiyon is "why is everything blurry and weird?" Part of that is structural — it runs at a lower resolution than DALL·E 3 or Midjourney, so it will never be razor-sharp. But a lot of bad output comes down to vague prompts, and that you can fix.

The trick is to stack three elements in order:

- **Subject (what):** `a cat`, `a futuristic city`
- **Action / context (doing what):** `sitting on a sofa`, `flying over the ocean`
- **Style / texture (what it looks like):** `watercolor`, `digital art`, `4k`, `cinematic lighting`

Compress those three into a single line and the model has a much easier time parsing your intent:

```
a futuristic city flying over the ocean, cinematic lighting, digital art
```

What *doesn't* work: long rambling descriptions and abstract mood words like "beautiful atmosphere" or "emotional vibe." The model can't ground those, so they just add noise.

Also worth internalizing — the same prompt produces different results every time. That's not a bug, it's how diffusion-style generators work. Don't judge a prompt on one attempt. Run it three or four times and pick the winner. Reddit's image-gen communities repeat this constantly: **iteration beats prompt-crafting.** Volume is the fastest path to a result you're happy with.

## Using Craiyon commercially: read this before you sell anything

If you're planning to use generated images in a side hustle, check the licensing rules before you ship anything. Get this wrong and you're risking a takedown, a banned account, or losing whatever you were monetizing.

Craiyon lets you use generated images on the free plan, but **free-tier images come with a Craiyon watermark.** To sell them, run them in ads, or use them in client deliverables cleanly, you need a paid plan (Supporter starts around $5/month). Commercial-use terms also change over time, so don't take my word for it — open Craiyon's Terms yourself before you build a business on top of it.

Practically, here's where Craiyon fits in a real workflow: it's an **ideation and rough-draft tool.** It's great for sketching a composition, testing a concept, or getting unstuck. It is not where you produce final, high-resolution deliverables.

When you need quality output, switch to:

- **DALL·E 3 (via ChatGPT)** — handles text rendering and fingers far more reliably, and nails complex compositions
- **Adobe Firefly** — trained on licensed data, so commercial rights are clean and explicit

The two-stage approach is the sane one: lock down your composition and concept for free in Craiyon, then finish the actual deliverable in a higher-tier tool. You keep costs near zero during exploration and only pay when you're producing something that ships.

## The honest comparison: Craiyon vs. DALL·E 3

To set expectations straight:

| | Craiyon | DALL·E 3 |
|---|---|---|
| Cost | Free (paid from ~$5/mo) | ChatGPT Plus, $20/mo |
| Signup | None | OpenAI account required |
| Quality | Low-res, rough | High, accurate text & layout |
| Best for | Trying it out, rough drafts | Polished, near-final work |

If you're optimizing for "I want to see something in 30 seconds with no commitment," Craiyon wins. If you're optimizing for output quality, it's not close — pay for DALL·E 3 or Firefly.

## Bottom line

Using DALL·E mini today means going to Craiyon and typing an English prompt. That's the whole skill. It's free, it needs no account, and you can run it as many times as you want — which makes it close to the ideal on-ramp for AI image generation.

Start by adding a style tag and generating a few times until the prompt structure clicks. Once you're at the point of actually monetizing the output, graduate to a tool with clear commercial licensing. That progression — free exploration first, paid production second — is the realistic route from "messing around with AI images" to "turning them into income."

## FAQ

**Does Craiyon work with non-English prompts?**
It accepts them, but accuracy drops hard. Write prompts like `a cat wearing a hat` in English for results that match your intent. If English isn't your strong suit, translate with DeepL first and paste the output.

**What's the difference between Craiyon and DALL·E 3?**
Craiyon is free, signup-free, and browser-based, but lower in quality and accuracy. DALL·E 3 requires ChatGPT Plus ($20/month) and is much stronger at rendering text and complex layouts. Pick Craiyon for convenience, DALL·E 3 for quality.

**Can I use Craiyon images commercially?**
Free-tier images carry a Craiyon watermark, and commercial use requires a paid plan (Supporter, ~$5/month and up). If you're using them for work, subscribe and verify the current commercial terms before publishing.

**How long does a Craiyon generation take?**
On the free plan, expect roughly 1–2 minutes per run, which returns nine images at once. Paid plans cut the wait and remove ads. During peak load, the free tier can take longer.
