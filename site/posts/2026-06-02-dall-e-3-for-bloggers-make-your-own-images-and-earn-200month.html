The 7-step workflow for producing blog images

The core loop breaks down into seven steps:

1. Log into ChatGPT (Plus required for DALL-E 3). 2. Open a new chat and ask it to generate an image. 3. Give it the article title *and* the target reader in the same message. 4. Specify the aspect ratio — 16:9 is the standard for featured images. 5. Pick the one or two outputs worth keeping. 6. Refine expression and color with follow-up prompts. 7. Download, convert to WebP, and upload to your blog.

Step 3 is the one most people skip, and it's the one that matters most. Compare "make a header image" with "Featured image for a beginner's guide to index-fund investing, aimed at readers in their twenties." Feeding DALL-E 3 that context roughly doubles the hit rate of usable results, because it's the same GPT-4 reasoning interpreting your request — not a keyword matcher.

A working prompt looks like this:

`` Create a featured image for a blog post titled "How to Start an AI Side Hustle." A smiling man in his twenties sitting at a laptop, bright room, soft morning light, flat-illustration style, 16:9. ``

Train yourself to always include five elements: subject, setting, light, style, and ratio. Specific beats abstract every time.

Five ways to tighten your prompts

DALL-E 3 leans on GPT-4's language understanding, which means it rewards natural, descriptive sentences. Unlike Midjourney's terse incantations, you get better output from plain, detailed prose.

Five habits that consistently raise quality:

  • Make the subject concrete. Not "a person" but "a woman in her thirties with short hair."
  • Name the light. "Morning daylight," "neon reflection," "overcast window light" — light direction shapes the entire mood.
  • Commit to one style. Watercolor, 3D render, or flat illustration — pick one. Mixing them muddies the result.
  • State the negatives. "No text in the image," "no visible faces." DALL-E 3 honors exclusions well.
  • Iterate in place. "Same image, but make the expression a smile" keeps composition stable while you tweak one variable.

If you publish images regularly, build three to five reusable templates of your own. That single move drops production time from about ten minutes per image to three. Browsing prompt collections that other bloggers share and adapting them to your niche is a fast way to seed those templates.

Commercial use and copyright: staying safe

Under OpenAI's terms, DALL-E 3 output is cleared for commercial use. Featured images, social posts, ebook illustrations — all fair game. Three guardrails keep you out of trouble.

Don't generate real people or named characters. Prompts that name celebrities are usually blocked outright, but conjuring a suspiciously specific look-alike and then monetizing it can stray into publicity-rights territory. Keep figures generic.

Don't include other brands' logos or trademarks. A coffee cup with a recognizable mark, a phone with a visible badge — anything carrying someone else's trademark is a risk you don't need to take.

Disclose AI generation. A short note in the caption or footer — "Image generated with DALL-E 3" — costs you nothing and buys you reader trust plus resilience against future terms changes. Provenance disclosure is trending toward the norm across most markets, so getting ahead of it is cheap insurance.

Which beats which: DALL-E 3 vs Midjourney for blogging
