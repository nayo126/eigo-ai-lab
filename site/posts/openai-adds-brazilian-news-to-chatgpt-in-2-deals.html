OpenAI Adds Brazilian News to ChatGPT in 2 Deals

Published: 2026-05-26 | Category: AI Industry

TL;DR

  • A new ChatGPT news partnership brings Grupo Folha and Grupo UOL journalism into ChatGPT with named attribution and source links.
  • The deal covers two of Brazil's largest publishers, extending OpenAI's content-licensing strategy into Portuguese-language news.
  • Publishers gain referral traffic and visibility; readers get sourced answers instead of unattributed summaries.

What happened

OpenAI announced a strategic content partnership with Grupo Folha and Grupo UOL, two of Brazil's biggest media companies. The agreement lets ChatGPT surface reporting from both groups when users ask about Brazilian news, current events, and related topics. Responses include attribution and links back to the original publishers.

Grupo Folha publishes *Folha de S.Paulo*, one of Brazil's most-read daily newspapers, along with the Datafolha polling institute and other titles. Grupo UOL operates UOL, one of the largest content and internet portals in Latin America, covering news, sports, finance, and entertainment. Together they reach a wide Portuguese-speaking audience across Brazil.

Under the partnership, content from these outlets can appear inside ChatGPT with transparency about where the information came from. OpenAI framed the deal around "trusted journalism," attribution, and clear sourcing rather than scraping or unlabeled reuse. The goal stated by OpenAI is to expand access to reliable news while pointing readers back to the publishers who produced it.

The move follows a pattern OpenAI has built over the past two years. The company has signed licensing and content arrangements with publishers including the Associated Press, Axel Springer, the Financial Times, News Corp, Le Monde, Hearst, and others. The Grupo Folha and Grupo UOL deals mark a notable expansion into Latin America and into Portuguese-language coverage specifically, a region OpenAI had addressed less directly than North America and Europe.

Financial terms were not disclosed in the announcement. As with prior agreements, the structure centers on attribution and surfacing within ChatGPT, with links driving readers to the source sites.

Why it matters

The partnership signals two shifts worth tracking.

First, the AI-versus-publisher relationship is moving from conflict toward negotiated supply. Several outlets, most prominently The New York Times, have sued OpenAI over training data and content use. At the same time, a growing list of publishers has chosen licensing deals instead. The Brazilian agreements show OpenAI continuing to build a paid, permissioned content layer rather than relying solely on open web crawling. For publishers weighing litigation against partnership, each new deal makes the licensing path more normal.

Second, this is a geographic and language expansion. Most high-profile AI news deals have involved English-language or major European outlets. Bringing in Brazil's leading publishers extends ChatGPT's sourced coverage to Portuguese, the language of more than 200 million people in Brazil alone. For OpenAI, localized, attributed news is a way to make ChatGPT more useful as a default information tool in markets where it competes with Google, Gemini, and local search.

The competitive angle is direct. Google has long sent referral traffic to publishers and has its own AI Overviews and licensing talks. Perplexity has built a publisher revenue-sharing program. By locking in trusted national outlets with attribution, OpenAI positions ChatGPT as a credible news surface and reduces the risk that AI answers strip publishers of traffic without credit. The attribution-and-links model is also a hedge against regulatory and legal pressure around AI use of copyrighted journalism.

For the broader market, the takeaway is that "trusted source" is becoming a product feature. As AI assistants compete on answer quality, having licensed, attributable journalism baked in is a differentiator that free-crawled models cannot safely match. Expect more regional deals to follow this template.

Try it yourself

  • Test attribution behavior: ask ChatGPT a current-events question about Brazil and check whether responses cite Folha de S.Paulo, UOL, or other named outlets with working links. Compare the same query against Gemini and Perplexity to see how each handles sourcing.
  • If you run a publication, audit your AI policy now. Decide whether you want to block AI crawlers, pursue a licensing deal, or join a revenue-share program like Perplexity's, and document the trade-offs before a vendor approaches you.
  • Treat ChatGPT as a research starting point, not a final source. When it surfaces a news claim, click through to the linked publisher to confirm the original reporting and date before citing it in your own work.
  • For SEO and content teams: monitor referral traffic from AI assistants in your analytics. Set up tracking for ChatGPT and other AI referrers so you can measure whether attribution deals actually send readers your way.
  • Watch for regional rollouts. If you build products for Portuguese-speaking users, test whether localized, sourced news improves your ChatGPT-powered features and plan around the languages OpenAI prioritizes next.
