"I keep hearing you can make images with AI, but I have no idea where to even start." If that's you, good news: there's no software to install and no cryptic English prompt to memorize. You can have your first image in a couple of minutes.

Here's the short version: DALL-E 3 is an image generator you drive by *talking to ChatGPT*. You don't need to be a designer. You type "draw me X" in plain language and it works.

Below: how to start for free, how to write prompts that get you what you actually pictured, and the commercial-use rules that matter if you're using these images to make money.

What DALL-E 3 Is — and How It Differs From Other Image AIs

!DALL-E 3 for Beginners - What DALL-E 3 Is and How It Differs

DALL-E 3 is OpenAI's image model, and its killer feature is that it's baked straight into ChatGPT. Where Stable Diffusion and Midjourney often turn into a contest over prompt precision, DALL-E 3 just reads the context of your conversation and renders it.

Under the hood, ChatGPT's language model takes your vague request and rewrites it into a detailed prompt before generating. So a loose line like "a cat studying in a cafe, rainy day, cozy mood" is enough — it fills in the composition and lighting for you.

A quick comparison of the big three:

  • DALL-E 3 — conversational. Decent at rendering text. The easiest on-ramp for beginners.
  • Midjourney — strongest on artistry and photorealism, but the Discord workflow takes getting used to.
  • Stable Diffusion — free and runs locally, but you have to set up the environment yourself.

If your goal is "make one good image fast, with zero friction," DALL-E 3 is the gentlest entry point.

Three Ways to Use It for Free

There are three main ways in. Pick based on cost and convenience.

1. Microsoft Designer / Copilot (free). A Microsoft account gets you DALL-E 3 at no cost. If you're undecided: start free in Microsoft Designer, then upgrade to ChatGPT Plus once you outgrow it. No wasted spend. The ChatGPT mobile app does the same thing, so you can crank out images on your commute.

The 5-Step Workflow

Here's the fastest path, using ChatGPT as the example. About three minutes start to finish.

1. Open ChatGPT (or Copilot). 2. Type "make an image of [X]" in plain English. 3. Wait a few seconds to ~15 seconds for the image. 4. Refine with follow-ups: "brighter," "make it portrait orientation," etc. 5. When you like it, right-click or long-press to save.

The key is *not* chasing perfection on the first try. Get a rough version out, then fix the details over the next few prompts. This back-and-forth refinement is exactly where DALL-E 3 shines.

For example, start with "a lemon orchard under a blue sky," then ask to "change it to dusk, warmer tones." The composition holds while the mood shifts. You can also call aspect ratio — "landscape (16:9)" or "square" — to size images for blog headers or social posts.

How to Prompt for the Image You Actually Want

DALL-E 3 runs fine on vague input, but four elements sharpen the result dramatically:

  • Subject — what you're drawing (e.g., a woman in her 30s, a laptop)
  • Scene / background — where (e.g., a window-side cafe, houseplants)
  • Style — the look (e.g., photographic, watercolor, flat design)
  • Light and color — the mood (e.g., soft morning light, warm tones)

Pack those four into one sentence. As a template:

`` [style], showing [subject] in [scene]. [lighting/color]. Example: Flat illustration of a man in his 20s working at a home desk. Bright morning light, blue and white palette. ``

What to avoid: asking for celebrity faces or specific brand logos. DALL-E 3 is built to block those. And if you want text inside the image, short English words hold up best — long passages still come out garbled, so it's safer to add text afterward in an image editor.

Commercial Use: What You Need to Know Before Selling

Plenty of people want to use DALL-E 3 images for blog headers, social media, or merch. This is the part to get right.

Under OpenAI's terms, images you generate are yours to use commercially. That said, conditions can differ by platform — Microsoft and others each have their own fine print — so always check the current terms for whatever service you're actually using. This isn't legal advice for your specific case, just a general flag: terms get revised without notice.

There's also an ongoing debate, especially in the US, that images generated purely by AI are hard to copyright. In practice that means if someone copies your image wholesale, you may have little legal footing to push back. If you're serious about using AI images in a business, the safe play is to treat the AI output as a base, then add your own text and layout so there's genuine original work on top.

Two rules cover most of the trouble: stay within the generation limits and terms, and don't use anyone's likeness or brand without permission. Do that and you'll dodge the vast majority of problems.

Wrapping Up

Boil it all down and using DALL-E 3 is just "ask ChatGPT in plain language." Make one image on Microsoft Designer's free tier to get a feel for it, then move to ChatGPT Plus when you want to produce at volume. Build prompts around the four elements — subject, scene, style, light — and re-check the terms each time you go commercial. Nail those basics and AI image generation becomes a serious tool for a side income. Make your first one today.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use DALL-E 3 images commercially?

Yes. Under OpenAI's terms, you own the images you generate with DALL-E 3 and can use them for sales, advertising, and more — blog headers, article illustrations, merch. The exception: generating real people's faces or existing brand logos violates the terms.

How many free images can I make with DALL-E 3?

The free ChatGPT tier caps you at a handful per day, and once you hit the limit you wait until the next day. To do more, ChatGPT Plus (~$20/month) lets you generate roughly a few dozen every three hours. For serious use, the paid plan is the realistic choice.

DALL-E 3 or Midjourney — which should I pick?

Beginners are better off with DALL-E 3. You generate just by talking to ChatGPT in plain language — no Discord, no English prompts. Midjourney wins on photorealism and artistic quality, so it's the pick for people chasing commercial-grade design.

Any tips when DALL-E 3 won't give me the image I want?

Don't just say "a cat" — say "an orange tabby curled up by a window at dusk, watercolor style." Layering subject, situation, and style sharpens the output. After it renders, keep refining in conversation ("brighter," "make it portrait") — each correction gets you closer.
