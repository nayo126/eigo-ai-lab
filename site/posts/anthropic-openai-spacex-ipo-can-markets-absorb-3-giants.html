Anthropic, OpenAI, SpaceX IPO: Can Markets Absorb 3 Giants?

Published: 2026-06-02 | Category: AI Industry

TL;DR

  • The three most valuable private tech firms — Anthropic, OpenAI and SpaceX — together carry valuations large enough to test how much new equity public markets can realistically absorb.
  • An Anthropic OpenAI IPO at current marks would rank among the biggest listings ever, raising questions about index weighting, liquidity and concentration risk.
  • For investors and builders, the takeaway is to watch funding structure, revenue multiples and lock-up terms rather than headline valuations.

What happened

The Economist examined a question now central to AI finance: can public stockmarkets absorb companies the size of Anthropic, OpenAI and SpaceX if they list? All three sit at the top of the private-company valuation table, each carrying marks that would have been unthinkable for a non-public firm a decade ago.

OpenAI has been valued in private rounds at figures approaching and exceeding the level of long-established public companies, driven by ChatGPT revenue and continued capital raises. Anthropic, maker of the Claude family of models including Sonnet 4.6, has raised repeatedly from large technology and cloud partners and now carries a valuation in the hundreds of billions. SpaceX, while not an AI company, sits in the same rarefied tier and is frequently grouped with the two model labs as a test case for how private capital has scaled ahead of public listings.

The core arithmetic is straightforward. When a single company is valued at a meaningful fraction of a trillion dollars, a public offering that floats even a slice of that equity represents one of the largest capital-raising events markets have seen. The article frames the concern less as whether demand exists and more as whether the structure of public markets — index funds, pension allocations, liquidity at the point of sale — can take on that much new supply without distortion.

These firms have so far stayed private far longer than earlier technology leaders, funded by private rounds, strategic investments from cloud providers, and structured deals rather than traditional venture equity. That has concentrated ownership among a small set of large backers and delayed the moment when ordinary investors can buy in directly.

Why it matters

The scale of these valuations changes the competitive and financial picture in several ways.

First, concentration. If one or more of these companies lists, it could quickly become a heavyweight in major indices. Index funds would be obliged to buy, channelling passive money into a small number of names whose revenues are still being established relative to their valuations. That raises the same concentration concerns already attached to the largest listed technology firms.

Second, the private-to-public gap. The longer these companies stay private, the more value accrues to early and strategic investors before public markets get access. When the listing finally comes, the pricing reflects rounds already completed at elevated marks, leaving less obvious upside for new buyers and more exposure to any correction in AI sentiment.

Third, the revenue-versus-valuation question. Model labs are spending heavily on compute and talent. Their valuations are built on projected growth rather than current profit. A public listing forces quarterly disclosure, and the gap between a private mark and what public investors will pay for disclosed fundamentals is where volatility tends to appear.

For the broader AI industry, the outcome matters because these firms set the pace. Anthropic and OpenAI compete directly on model capability, enterprise contracts and developer adoption. How their financing evolves — more private rounds, structured debt, or an eventual IPO — shapes how much capital flows into the sector and how much pricing discipline the market imposes.

Try it yourself

Readers following AI markets or building on these platforms can take concrete steps today.

1. Track the funding structure, not just the valuation. When a new Anthropic or OpenAI round is announced, note who is investing (cloud partners, sovereign funds, venture firms) and whether it is equity, debt or a structured deal. The structure signals more about risk than the headline number. 2. Compare valuation to disclosed revenue where available. For any AI firm with reported figures, calculate a rough revenue multiple and compare it against listed software peers. This gives a baseline for judging whether a private mark is aggressive. 3. If you build on Claude or ChatGPT, diversify model dependencies. Keep at least one alternative integrated (for example, run a Claude path and a GPT-5 path behind the same interface) so a pricing or availability shift at one vendor does not break your product. 4. Watch for lock-up and float details if a listing is announced. The percentage of shares actually floated and the lock-up period determine real liquidity and short-term price behaviour far more than the nominal valuation. 5. Set a simple alert for index-inclusion news. If a major AI firm lists and enters a benchmark index, passive flows can move the price independently of fundamentals — useful context whether you invest or simply price your own compute costs.
